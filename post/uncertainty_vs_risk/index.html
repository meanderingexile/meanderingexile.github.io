Uncertainty and Risk

 Jun 21, 2022    6 min read
decisions

The fundamental difference is pretty simple: when risk kills you, you see it coming. When uncertainty kills you, you don’t.

This is Knight’s distinction of risk vs. uncertainty from 1921. In risk the outcome is uncertain, but the probability distribution is known, while in uncertainty both the outcomes and their distribution are unknown. Most of what we’d call risk in day-to-day life is actually uncertainty. We don’t know the probability distributions for things that threaten us. Even if the data’s available, most people don’t think about accident statistics before they embark on a road trip or board a plane.

More data helps you manage risk better, or at least gives you the opportunity to do so: if you interpret it correctly and make use of it. This is the part where our poor brains often struggle, with our sense of payoffs distorted by the prospect-theory curve, and our inability to parse statistics and numerical data.

From an evolutionary perspective, we should probably cut our brains a little slack here: the idea of having large amounts of data about events you’ve never experienced is, historically, pretty novel. We do seem to have some intrinsic sense of probability, an neural encoding for likelyhood vs reward, but this is something that we would only have built up over time. This means events that we’re deeply familiar with, and have experienced in a social context. Everything else is the domain of uncertainty.

We can see this by looking at our cousins, rhesus monkeys. Like us, they keep track of a risk-vs-reward curve, mentally computing something that resembles expected value. This was demonstrated in an experiment where they were shown bars with varying proportions of red or blue, reflecting the chance that the monkey would be rewarded with juice for choosing that option. Crucially, when the middle of the bar was obscured, the rhesus monkeys (like humans) exhibited ambiguity aversion, preferring options where more information was available (Glimcher, 2014). As the monkeys became comfortable with the experiment, over the course of several weeks, this bias faded.

I should warn here that everything below this is speculation. This example is really interesting to me, however. From an ecological perspective, the simplest explanation for this is just evolutionary risk aversion. Evolutionary survival is first and foremost a game of avoiding ruin, not hitting jackpots, and while some risks get rewarded, the brain’s natural bias is toward avoiding potential risks, not correctness.

When we think about the uncertainty/risk distinction, though, we can view this in a new light. The monkey in the experiment is playing a gambling game, a slot machine where they receive or do not receive juice. At least initially, however, the monkey does not know this. Even as they start to learn that more blue means more chance of juice, they do not know what’s hiding under the hidden section of bar. It could (and in fact, does) simply have more information about the juice odds: or it could hold a trap, a hidden third option where the monkey is suddenly and violently eaten. The monkey doesn’t have access to the true set of objects in the event space, and in the real world, neither do we. Even a particularly bright monkey, armed with an advanced knowledge of probability, wouldn’t fare better here, because the question is one of uncertainty: the only way to know that the hidden zone didn’t contain an previously-unseen danger is to take it on faith from the strange, white lab coat-adorned apes who are holding them prisoner. Monkey midbrain wisely remains unconvinced.

Like us in life, however, the monkey has no choice but to play the game: and so they choose, and learn, and choose again. As time goes on, they receive more or less juice, but remain uneaten, and their confidence grows that this really is the whole game, and so they begin to treat it, correctly, as a game of risk.

This also provides an interesting perspective on one of our species’ favorite heuristics: imitation, or mimesis. When in doubt, do what the person next to you is doing. Mimesis from an ecological rationality perspective is something that, I think, is understudied. Imitating others helps us learn skills and knowledge, but it does much more. It’s an incredibly efficient way of taking advantage of the knowledge of others: since you and I typically want the same things, I only need to know that you want something to know that I should probably want it too. It provides a form of command and control, allowing humans to synchronize, flock, and swarm in many situations with little deliberation.

Last but not least, it provides us with valuable information when data is thin on the ground. When we see others do things, we see how they’ve fared. Of course, this tells us very little about causality or risk: in a modern context, faithful imitation of a lottery winner is unlikely to make you rich. In a more dangerous world, though, where all options are fraught with uncertainty, imitation reduces it. It provides, if nothing else, a prototype: an example of behaviour that at least can succeed, which may be more than can be said for other options. If your friends jump off a bridge into the river, it may still be a bad idea to follow: you don’t know which one might have missed a rock by a hairsbreadth, and that same rock might be waiting for you below. But you know that the jump is at least survivable, and depending on your needs and options, that just might be compelling enough.

I find it useful to think about this, including the biology and evolutionary history, because I think it helps me understand myself and others a little better. Risk and uncertainty are easy to confuse. This can happen consciously, as a failure of analysis: collecting more data won’t help you if your problem is fundamentally one of uncertainty.

It can also happen unconsciously: you may know that the problem is with one, but feel the other. Those feelings are real: The fear of uncertainty. The urge sometimes to avoid risk, and sometimes to gamble. The pressure to imitate and conform, and the stress of diverging from that. These things are powerful. Knowing them, and where they come from, helps you to recognize and manage them: this is often crucial for understanding how to convince and reassure others. You have to know what the problem is before you can solve it, and what’s making people uncomfortable often isn’t what they say.

At the same time, it helps you to understand yourself, and your own heuristics and feelings around risk. This lets you be gentle with yourself when you know they’re incorrect. The ancient warning signs aren’t epistemic sins, heresies of bias to be rooted out, but old friends and protectors who sometimes need to be calmed and comforted, like a loyal guard dog startled by a noise outside.

One final thought: knowing how your instincts go wrong has a dual to it, which is that it also lets you appreciate situations where following your intuitive risk judgements might be wise. Sometimes, when the dog barks, you should check the door.

Refs:

Glimcher, Paul W. Neuroeconomics. Elsevier, 2014.
