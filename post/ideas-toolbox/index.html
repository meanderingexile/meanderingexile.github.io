A Messy Toolbox of Ideas

 Apr 17, 2022    18 min read

Finding Rocks To Hit Stuff With

I’ve been interested in strategy, as an abstract concept, since I was a teenager. This probably grew out of a sense of weakness, a feeling of wanting to have an effect on the world, and not being unable to. Because I was cognitively-oriented (nerdy) that expressed itself through fantasies of cleverness and strategic brilliance. By knowing things others didn’t, by understanding things they didn’t, by secret ways and secret knowledge, I’d be able to achieve my ends, even if I wasn’t entirely clear on what those ends actually were. I didn’t end up finding the strategic Philosopher’s Stone, but it ended up sending me off down a bunch of interesting rabbit holes, and exposed me to a bunch of neat ideas I probably wouldn’t have otherwise encountered.

This post is an overview of a bunch of areas that, for whatever reason, have clicked with my brain and given me a feeling of making progress in understanding the human world: systems theory and complexity science, decision science, microeconomics and game theory, cognitive neuroscience and evolutionary psychology, military strategy, big/world history, and practical philosophy and modern therapy.

There’s a particular aspect of the nerd brain that loves over-categorizing things (early exposure to D&D or video game tech trees intensifies this condition), so I should emphasize that the schema doesn’t matter that much. It’s fuzzy, and ideas may naturally bridge across categories; in some ways that’s desirable, because it provides a way of linking between ideas, and getting a leg up on the learning process. Swap out subsections or modules as you see fit, and augment them with the specific skills you need to do wherever you find your calling. Engineering, business, medicine, or art all have their own specific skills and expertise that can’t be derived from meditation.

Mental Models Are Cringe

Cedric Chin has a good piece on how abstract mental models, divorced from context, don’t do much to communicate actual practitioner skill in applied domains (https://commoncog.com/blog/the-mental-model-fallacy/). The followup, on actually improving decision making broadly, is worth reading as well (https://commoncog.com/blog/a-framework-for-putting-mental-models-to-practice/).

Given that, I don’t want to create another mental models listicle. It’s easy to become fixated on collecting tools for their own sake, tinkering with methods and the feeling of satisfaction it brings while time and results slip away. It’s easy to do this in software, but it applies to abstract concepts too.

We should start then with a warning from me to my present and future self, and to you, if you’re reading this: there is no secret knowledge, no secret key to victory, and watch out for ends disguised as means. The work is what matters, and if a framework, a tool, an idea doesn’t serve you, throw it away. Take care of the people who matter to you, and do the things you want to do. Life is short.

With that warning in mind, ideas are still useful, and fun. This is an attempt to gather a few of them: a bunch of different, interesting ways of thinking about the world, and the ones that I find myself thinking about a lot. This document grew out of some notes to myself, where I was trying to cluster and link a bunch of different reading I’d been doing in the last few years. I’m chucking it online, in very slightly edited form, in case it’s of interest to you as well, whoever you may be: if part of it resonates, then maybe there’s some other stuff that’s worth googling as well.

Strategy for Dilettantes

Strategy is about agents: people, or groups of people. It’s also about prediction: fuzzy and imperfect as it may be, you have to have some way of judging what end different actions will lead you to. Some roads lead to success, some lead to ruin. It’s often impossible to known which one is which, so we make our best guesses. The world is also an incomprehensibly vast and complicated place. We construct models, which is a fancy way of saying we choose some aspects to focus on and some aspects to ignore.

For every one of them, there’s an academic who’s made it their life’s work, analyzing things at a microscopic level of detail. We don’t want to go that far; we have a lot of ground to cover in a short life, and in many cases, the finest details are unnecessary. If you truly come across a problem that requires a dissertation’s level of technical detail to solve it correctly, then that is out of scope for this discussion, and I can only wish you good luck.

At the same time, reality is complex and precise. A superficial, airport-literature analysis of almost any topic at best doesn’t give you much to work with, and at worst simply acts as a cover for confirmation of your existing biases. The details matter- you may not be an expert, but you should probably be able to have an engaging conversation with one over a beer.

At the end of the day, you need to be able to make predictions about the world; testable predictions, since you will be putting your future, wallet, and skin on the line.

Enough caveats. Let’s talk details.

Complexity science and systems theory

Systems theory is, in a sense, a broad theory of how the universe works: understanding the dynamics of how groups of interacting, interrelated, but distinct parts unfold. As a result, it’s both fascinatingly general and frustratingly vague. We divide the world up into boxes, and try to understand how those bodies interact. In one extreme, we have differential equations derived from the laws of physics and extremely well-defined boundaries; the classic box diagrams of electrical engineering and control theory. On the other extreme, we have fuzzy causal loop diagrams, where the dynamics are complex and tangled enough that we can only vaguely indicate that one aspect is linked with another, and give a sense of direction. Human systems are inevitably close to the latter, though tools like operations research and systems dynamics (systems theory’s less hippie, mathematically-oriented cousin) try to hammer them into something closer to the former. Even if we can’t explicitly write equations, knowing the recurring patterns and archetypes is useful: we can see a feedback loop and understand the basic shapes it’s likely to exhibit without writing computer code or knowing the feedback gain. This in turn connects us to basic ideas of constraints, interfaces, and systems engineering, as we try to influence or build systems to our benefit.

Complexity theory essentially builds on the ideas of system theory to look at systems with more complex interactions. When you’re talking about an ecosystem, an economy, or a brain, we’re no longer looking at a small number of linear feedback loops, but a vast space of possibility. One day, this work may provide a unifying theory for understanding these types of systems; for now, it gives us a wide range of metaphors, toy models, and interesting questions. The starting point is nonlinear dynamics and chaos, which helps us understand how even simple-looking interactions can lead to complicated and hard-to-predict consequences. From this we get emergence, where groups of entities following locally simple rules produce unexpected large-scale behaviour that’s resistant to reductive analysis. Often the structure of the relationships between those entities is what matters: hence we find our way to aspects of graph theory and network science. As they interact with each other and influence each other’s behavior, information flows around the system: this link to information theory gives us tools for trying to study these systems, but also in turn provides connections to computer science; we turn ourselves upside down, and rather than looking at information as emitted from the system, we look at how the system processes information.

This is abstract, often technical, and frankly not often practical: though there are circumstances where you might, if so inclined, sketch a nonlinear dynamical equation or build an agent-based model of a system interest, most of the time this stuff is simply a source of analogy and toy models. These analogies recur so frequently, however, that I think they’re worth taking time to understand: they’re as close as we have to a nascent theory of how the world, on the scale you and I care about, works.

Decision theory

Decision theory is the study of how we make decisions, and how we theoretically should, two areas that are usually radically at odds with each other. Part of this is Bayesian reasoning, Tetlockian forecasting, practical gambling, and statistical prediction. When the conditions are right, and the data is available, there are correct ways of reasoning and making inferences that are superior to the alternatives. If you follow the rabbit hole, this gets you into connections to machine learning, statistical Markov decision process theory, and theoretical work that links out to AI and neuroscience like the free energy principle.

On the other hand, we have models for how we actually make decisions. Historically, this has focused on the Heuristics and Biases school of Kahneman, and it’s worth having a passing familiarity with the various cognitive biases embedded in our brains. As time goes on, however, more and more of the results the project relied on have fallen to the replication crisis. More fundamentally, we’ve come to understand how what looks like a bias or error in a lab context makes evolutionary or practical context. Gerd Gigerenzer and Gary Klein have complimentary sets of tools for thinking about practical time-constrained decisions, and some preliminary tools for improving them.

Microeconomics and game theory

Like everything, we’re thinking about decisions. Economics gives us tools for thinking about agents making decisions in the context of resources and scarcity.

Probably more than anything else on this list, this one is political. The mix I have reflects my interests and biases: I have a lot of friends who are Marxists, for instance, but I don’t put a ton of stock in Marxist economic theory.

I do think it’s worth having a grounding in basic microeconomics: ideas of supply and demand, simple consumer models, ways of modelling firms, technologies, and markets. This stuff is the ur-example of “all models are wrong, some models are useful” but still good to know. Beyond that, it’s worth having a passing familiarity with principal-agent theory, ideas of information asymmetry, and contract theory/mechanism design. Even 30 years on Varian and Shapiro’s Information Rules does a good job of applying that stuff to an internet context, which is probably relevant.

As you start understanding how agents try to screw one another, you in turn get to game theory, and how circumstances can drive competition or cooperation. This then breaks out into public choice theory, some Austrian economics (remarkably prescient in how it looks at things like distributed information flows), and political economy (Elinor Ostrom’s work in particular is a good counterbalance to just thinking about things in terms of pure markets).

Cognitive neuroscience and evolutionary psychology

As a human being, most of the sociotechnical systems you’re involved with will be other human beings. Also, you’re one. All of you are slightly confused savannah apes, trapped in the middle of a blazing-fast technocapital acceleration you didn’t necessarily ask for. Have fun!

More seriously, understanding the neurological hardware that you and those around you are using to make decisions. Really basic things, like your need for sleep, motivation, and emotional regulation can make far more sense if you understand what’s actually going on inside your head. Thinking of things as biological information processing puts a human side on the abstract agents from decision theory or economics, and you can start to understand how certain aspects (like prospect theory) emerge at a very low level.

For this stuff to make sense, you also really want the evolutionary context. Many of the things that you or others do may not be rational from an abstract decision maker’s perspective, or for someone trying to thrive in a modern environment, but they do make sense for an organism in the environment you evolved in that is trying to optimize for survival. This in turn links back to ecological rationality in decision science models.

The other thing that evolutionary theory- real evolutionary theory, not the trite misunderstanding of fitness you still see in pop culture- helps you appreciate and understand when people are seemingly working at cross purposes to themselves. This includes you, dealing with conflicting submodules or drives within your own brain. It’s true, you are a stupendous badass, the pinnacle of four and a half billion years of evolution. You’re also a gigantic pile of biological hacks, hotfix piled on top of hotfix in a desperate attempt to make it to the next generation. Just knowing this can be a useful antidote to the frustration that things don’t seem to fit some coherent, grand design.

After economics, this is probably the second-most controversial entry on the list, because evo psych has a way of making enemies: those who see it as conflicting with deeply held religious views about human nature on one hand, an those who see it as simply a justification for existing power hierarchies on the other. This is especially true when it comes to things like gender roles, which given the importance of sexual selection, are a central point of evolutionary psychology.

At the end of the day, you have to decide which aspects you’re willing to take or leave, and which ones make sense to you, where you feel the evidence actually holds. I try to navigate this through two principles. First, through appreciation for the vast complexity that human beings are capable of, and the variation of human minds. Your evolutionary/genetic legacy is a starting point, not an end. Second, the naturalistic fallacy is, well, a fallacy. The fact that something- a drive, a heuristic, a behaviour- isn’t morally good because it’s natural any more than cancer is. Scientific theories let you predict and expect: what you choose to do with that is up to you, and on your head.

Military strategy

This is one I’ve gone back and forth on in recent years, but think I’ve settled on leaving it in. From our perspective, strategy is about designing and using complex human organizations to defeat other, complex, problem-solving organizations under adversarial circumstances. Conflict is a common feature of our world, and it’s worth having some tools for understanding it.

The whole area is a bit eclectic: the field still frequently likes to make a strong Eastern/Western distinction, so you’ll get one series of readings that trace a link from the Greeks and Romans through to Napoleon and the modern West, and another that inevitably start with Sun Tzu and meanders through (typically) Mao Zedong, with a diversion through the military cultures of Japan. Real macro theories here are limited, though Clausewitz and Boyd are both worth plenty of attention. Instead, as technology changes, you’ll get a lot of snapshots of individual cases of problem solving (Chinese state warfare, naval power in the 19th century, 20th century air power, etc.) with some unifying themes running through them. In the 20th century, Schelling’s game theory links out to economic game theory, and provides some good context for understanding conflict more broadly, and nuclear strategy in particular.

Modern materials (like Grey’s Modern Strategy, for instance) can be dense and technical, since they’re primarily aimed at practitioners, but it’s worth spending some time to see how these ideas get implemented in the real, modern world that you’re most familiar with. Military history (when it’s good, not war porn) is important here, to understand what very abstract concepts actually look like when people are running around screaming and bleeding.

World history

For history, I try to drive things to two extremes: big, and small.

Tanner Greer has a good line: “history is the data of social science” (https://scholars-stage.org/a-study-guide-for-human-society-part-i/). History, small-scale history, with the details and complexity and ambiguity, is the bedrock that keeps you from being a bullshit artist. The things that you care about in the future will happen just like things in the past- done by real people, operating in conditions of imperfect information, with naps and meals and bathroom breaks.

This is very personal, depending on your background and interests. Again, I like Greer’s starting point: pick three areas of interest to you. Spread them out in space and time- make sure one is non-Western, one is modern, one is ancient. Greer suggests 4-6 books on each region as a starting point, which is pretty fair. Then branch out to other periods. If you have gaping holes in your knowledge (I once realized that my entire mental model of WWI was essentially the final season of Blackadder, for instance) then aim to fill them. The same principle of BS detection applies to other fields as well: for everything above (evolutionary biology, military history, economics) you want to be reading relevant history and biography: the details of the events and the models, not just the high-level products.

That’s a lot of reading. That should probably be around 80% of your time.

The other 20%, it’s worth spending some time on things that try to provide large-scale theories. I find Turchin’s cliodynamics stuff interesting here, since it applies a lot of the system dynamics/complexity approaches to historical phenomena. Macro political science theories cam be interesting, and overlap heavily with economics and strategy, though you need to be careful about understanding when they’re too low-resolution to actually be useful.

Finally, I think a little bit of big history is valuable: Sagan’s Cosmos is dated but a classic. Sapiens was popular in this area a few years ago, though it’s also worth reading the critiques. Specific texts on primatology and human history are also relevant here: it’s interesting to think about the 99% of human history before our current technological civilization.

In particular, I like David Christian’s Maps of Time, even as some of the details change. This one is interesting both for the ideas (negentropy and intensifying complexity are really fascinating ideas about the fundamental thermodynamic nature of life and history, especially in our current, accelerating times) but also just for the perspective. Deep time is a hard thing to grasp, but when you approach it, it can give you a sense of vastness and scope, a glimpse of the sublime.

Practical philosophy and therapy

This collection is a toolbox, and this section reflects that. I assume that you have a moral philosophy and purpose of your own; this is ideas I’ve found useful, or think I’ve found useful, for pursuing mine. This section is maybe best thought of as ways to build a good life, and to deal with the bumps in the road along the way.

It’s worth it to think about what a good life means to you. Classics like Man’s Search For Meaning are worth it here, Montaigne, and potentially religious or spiritual texts that align with your own traditions and inclinations. There’s also a growing body of work in performance philosophy and performance psych: understanding what experiences and circumstances help make us happiest, and how mental framing, self-talk, and other tools can help us actually get there. Some of this is pretty banal or obvious, and much of it agrees with ideas from classic philosophy. Again, overlapping perspectives are useful.

Two specific traditions I’ve found both useful and overplayed are the Helenistic Stoics and Buddhist writings in the (quite divergent) Zen and Tantric traditions. Both of them are different, but related ways of dealing with the struggles and suffering inherent to a world that isn’t under our control. They both suffer from substantial PR problems: the very dictionary equates the Stoics with an unhealthy, muted emotional repression, while Buddhism in the west is often a mix of orientalist caricature and a syncretic hippie aesthetic. These both have aspects that are less useful, or even actively undesirable (see https://vividness.live/meditation-risks, for instance). As materialist ideological pirates, though, we can find perspectives and techniques that are useful.

A useful link for me was Donald Robertson’s contemporary work on the link between Stoicism and cognitive behavioral therapy. This was interesting for two reasons for me: one, because I understood better that what the Stoics and ancient Buddhists were doing was a form of therapy, and that what I was getting out of the techniques was therapeutic. This lead me to spending more time with CBT materials and techniques. I still find the two complimentary: the CBT materials are often more direct for specific emotional challenges, but the more philosophical works have a helpful, holistic perspective, whereas modern therapy is often inherently medicalizing.

Lately, I’ve been branching out a little more into ideas from dialectical behaviour therapy and internal family systems therapy. The former has more of a focus on integrating mindfulness practices. The latter is a way of talking about life history and trauma in a way that makes a lot of sense to me from a neurological perspective: discrete clusters of associations and responses that present themselves as modular sub-selves.

Like with self-help literature, there’s a lot of this stuff for a reason: a ton of it is chaff, but also sometimes you just have to find things that click. A book that’s life-changing for one person in a certain place and time will seem banal to someone else. Explore, and see what clicks.

Doing The Homework

This started as me trying to compile some notes from my last couple years of reading, and instead turned into a sort of introduction for a book that doesn’t exist. Reading older blogs, there’s two things I always found frustrating: when someone brought up interesting ideas, but didn’t provide enough detail or sources for me to follow up, and bloggers that promised future, interesting-sounding posts that never came. I think we’ve got the vague part covered above; for the latter, I’ll say that I have a lot more notes that need writing up, with more depth, book citations, and galaxy-brain connections between ideas I probably don’t fully understand.
